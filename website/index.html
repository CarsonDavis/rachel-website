About Me

I am a Postdoctoral Fellow at the Lunar and Planetary Institute (LPI/USRA) with a Ph.D. in Space and Planetary Science from the University of Arkansas. My research focuses on understanding Martian water cycles, salt behavior, and potential habitability through experimental studies and Earth analog investigations.

My work combines laboratory experiments using Mars simulation chambers, field studies in analog environments like the Atacama Desert and Colorado Plateau, and geochemical modeling to understand how liquid water might exist and persist on Mars.

My Journey to Planetary Science

Early Inspiration (Age 4)

My passion for space began in preschool during "space month" in April. After making paper astronaut helmets, hunting for "Moon rocks," and visiting the Neil Armstrong Museum in Wapakoneta, Ohio, I was inspired by Neil Armstrong and decided I wanted to be an astronaut.

Discovering Geology

A few years later, I developed a passion for rocks and geology. When I learned I could combine being an astronaut with studying rocks on other planets, I knew that was my calling for life. As I like to say: "I like rocks, and whether they are on Earth or other planetary bodies, I will happily study them!"
